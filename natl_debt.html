The discrepancy between the value of gold in the market and the implied value based on the US debt and gold reserves in 1971 is due to the abandonment of the gold standard and the fixed price of gold set by the government.

Before 1971, the US adhered to the Bretton Woods system, which fixed the value of the US dollar to a specific amount of gold ($35 per ounce). This fixed rate meant that the government was committed to exchanging dollars for gold at that price.

However, the market value of gold was different due to various factors such as supply, demand, speculation, and economic conditions. Despite the fixed price set by the government, the market demand for gold often surpassed this fixed value due to various global economic shifts and inflationary pressures.

The $35 per ounce price was a government-fixed rate established for international transactions and the valuation of the US dollar against gold under the Bretton Woods system. The market value, driven by various economic factors, often diverged significantly from this fixed rate.
