You can use a non-KYC Bitcoin wallet in the U.S., but the rules surrounding how you acquire and exchange Bitcoin without KYC are complex and ever-changing
. 
Here's a breakdown of the current situation and the key considerations: 
Wallets themselves often don't require KYC 
  • Many popular non-custodial wallets like MetaMask, Trust Wallet, and Exodus don't inherently require you to provide personal identification information.
  • Non-custodial wallets give you full control over your private keys, meaning you are responsible for securing your Bitcoin. 
Exchanges and ramps have KYC requirements 
  • Centralized exchanges: Major exchanges like Coinbase and Binance are required to implement KYC protocols to comply with Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF) regulations. This means you will need to provide personal details and verify your identity before you can use these platforms to buy, sell, or trade Bitcoin.
  • Decentralized exchanges (DEXs): Some DEXs like Uniswap and PancakeSwap don't require KYC, according to Koinly. These platforms facilitate peer-to-peer trading and typically don't hold your funds, but they may have other limitations.
  • Peer-to-peer (P2P) platforms: Some P2P platforms like Hodl Hodl and Bisq allow you to trade Bitcoin directly with other users, often without KYC. However, you'll need to negotiate deals and bring your own wallet. 
Legal and regulatory landscape 
  • Evolving Regulations: The US government is increasingly focused on regulating the cryptocurrency space. Entities involved in the exchange, transfer, or custody of cryptocurrencies are typically classified as Money Services Businesses (MSBs) and must register with FinCEN.
  • Risks of using non-KYC platforms: Using non-KYC platforms carries increased risks, including potential vulnerability to scams, regulatory crackdowns, and difficulty in tax reporting.
  • Tax obligations: In the US, digital assets are considered property for tax purposes, and you are required to report all cryptocurrency transactions (sales, trades, etc.) to the IRS, regardless of whether you used a KYC or non-KYC platform. 
Important considerations 
  • Security: Whether or not you use a KYC platform, always prioritize the security of your Bitcoin. Use strong passwords, enable two-factor authentication, and consider using a hardware wallet for storing larger amounts.
  • Legal Compliance: Be aware of the evolving regulatory landscape in the US. While using non-KYC wallets might not be illegal in itself, participating in certain transactions on non-KYC platforms could raise red flags with authorities or be in violation of regulations.
  • Tax Reporting: Regardless of the type of wallet or platform you use, you are legally obligated to report your cryptocurrency transactions to the IRS. 
In summary 
You can technically use a non-KYC Bitcoin wallet in the US. However, acquiring Bitcoin and exchanging it for fiat currency often necessitates using platforms that are legally obligated to implement KYC procedures. Using non-KYC platforms may offer privacy but comes with inherent risks and potential legal complexities. It's crucial to be aware of the regulations and associated risks and to prioritize the security of your assets.
